Congratulations! You have completed the initial security configuration of your AWS account.

* Created an administrator group.

---

# Section 1 — Your Daily AWS Workflow

From this point forward, your normal workflow should be simple and consistent.

Instead of signing in with the root account, use your IAM administrator account for routine tasks.

Your daily workflow should look like this:

```text
Open your IAM sign-in URL
          ↓
Sign in as your IAM administrator
          ↓
Complete your AWS tasks
          ↓
Sign out when finished
```

Examples of everyday tasks include:

* Creating Amazon S3 buckets
* Launching Amazon EC2 instances
* Building VPCs
* Configuring CloudWatch
* Creating IAM roles
* Working on the projects in this handbook

These activities should be performed with your IAM administrator account, not the root user.

---

# Section 2 — When Should You Use the Root User?

Your root account should remain protected and rarely used.

Examples of situations where you may need the root user include:

* Certain account ownership tasks
* Specific billing or account-level operations
* Recovery procedures that require root credentials
* Closing the AWS account
* Managing root account settings

If a task can be completed with your IAM administrator account, use the administrator account instead.

A simple rule to remember is:

```text
Daily work = IAM administrator

Account ownership tasks = Root user
```

---

# Section 3 — Protecting Your Administrator Account

Your IAM administrator account now has broad administrative permissions.

Treat it with the same level of care you would give to other important accounts.

Always:

* Use a strong, unique password.
* Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) if you have not already done so.
* Sign out when you finish working.
* Avoid using public or shared computers.
* Keep your credentials private.
* Store passwords securely in a trusted password manager.

Never:

* Share your password.
* Share your MFA device.
* Send credentials through unsecured email or messaging apps.
* Save passwords in plain-text files.

---

# Section 4 — Managing Your Administrator Account

As your AWS experience grows, you may need to make changes to your administrator account.

Examples include:

* Changing your password
* Updating your MFA device
* Changing your display name
* Rotating credentials when appropriate
* Reviewing your group membership
* Reviewing recent account activity

Perform these tasks through the IAM console while signed in as your administrator.

---

# Section 5 — Common Operational Mistakes

New AWS users often make simple mistakes after creating their administrator account.

Avoid the following:

### Mistake 1 – Continuing to Use the Root User

After setup, always begin by signing in with your IAM administrator.

---

### Mistake 2 – Forgetting the IAM Sign-In URL

Store the URL securely.

Bookmark it in your browser if appropriate.

---

### Mistake 3 – Losing Administrator Credentials

Record:

* IAM username
* Sign-in URL
* Password manager entry
* MFA recovery information

Keep these records secure.

---

### Mistake 4 – Creating Unnecessary Administrator Accounts

Create additional administrators only when there is a legitimate operational requirement.

---

### Mistake 5 – Ignoring MFA

Enable MFA for privileged accounts as soon as practical.

---

### Mistake 6 – Leaving the Console Signed In

Always sign out after completing your work, especially on shared or public devices.

---

# Section 6 — Account Health Review

Before beginning your AWS projects, perform a final review.

Confirm the following:

### Root Account

* Strong password
* MFA enabled
* No root access keys
* Used only when required

### Administrators Group

* Group exists
* AdministratorAccess attached

### Administrator User

* Username confirmed
* Console access working
* Member of the Administrators group
* Password stored securely
* MFA planned or enabled

### IAM Sign-In

* Sign-in URL saved
* Bookmark created if desired
* Successful sign-in tested
