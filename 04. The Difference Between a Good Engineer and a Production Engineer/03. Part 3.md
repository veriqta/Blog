

## Part 3: How Great Engineers Respond When Everything Goes Wrong

*"Every engineer enjoys success. Production engineers are defined by how they respond to failure."*

In Part 1, we explored the mindset that separates good engineers from production engineers.

In Part 2, we examined how great engineers make difficult decisions when there isn't a perfect answer.

But every production engineer eventually encounters the same reality.

Despite careful planning...

Despite testing...

Despite automation...

Despite years of experience...

Something will eventually fail.

Servers become unavailable.

Databases slow down.

Deployments introduce unexpected behavior.

Third-party services become unreachable.

Cloud providers experience outages.

Production is unpredictable.

The difference between an ordinary engineer and a production engineer is not whether they experience failure.

It is how they respond when failure arrives.

---

# Why Every Production Engineer Eventually Faces Failure

Many engineers spend years trying to avoid failure.

Production engineers prepare for it.

Not because they expect disaster every day.

Because they understand that modern software systems are incredibly complex.

Thousands of services.

Millions of requests.

Distributed infrastructure.

External dependencies.

Multiple teams.

Changing business requirements.

No engineer can completely eliminate failure from systems of this scale.

The goal is not perfection.

The goal is resilience.

Production engineers understand that failure is not the opposite of engineering.

Failure is part of engineering.

---

# The First 15 Minutes of a Production Incident

The first few minutes of a production incident often determine everything that follows.

Unfortunately, they are also the moments when panic spreads the fastest.

Alerts begin firing.

Customers report problems.

Executives ask for updates.

Multiple engineers start proposing solutions.

Everyone wants to help.

That is exactly when mistakes happen.

Experienced production engineers slow the situation down.

Not the recovery.

The thinking.

Instead of immediately changing systems, they first answer a few critical questions.

What exactly is failing?

Who is affected?

How widespread is the impact?

When did it begin?

What changed recently?

The first objective is understanding the situation.

Not fixing something that might not be broken.

---

# Staying Calm Under Pressure

Remaining calm is not a personality trait.

It is a professional skill.

Production engineers understand that stress reduces decision quality.

When people panic:

They skip verification.

They make assumptions.

They introduce additional failures.

They communicate poorly.

They overlook important evidence.

Calm engineers think clearly.

They separate facts from assumptions.

They prioritize customer impact.

They focus on one problem at a time.

Calmness spreads through teams just as quickly as panic.

Leadership during incidents often begins with emotional stability.

---

# Thinking Before Acting

One of the most dangerous habits during outages is changing multiple things at once.

Someone restarts services.

Someone changes configuration.

Someone scales infrastructure.

Someone updates DNS.

Someone deploys new code.

Minutes later...

Nobody knows which action changed the outcome.

Production engineers avoid random actions.

Every change should answer a question.

Every action should have a purpose.

Every decision should improve understanding.

Fast does not mean rushed.

Fast means deliberate.

---

# Communication During Major Incidents

Technical recovery is only part of incident management.

Communication is equally important.

Customers need updates.

Leadership needs accurate information.

Support teams need guidance.

Other engineers need coordination.

Production engineers communicate clearly.

Not emotionally.

Not defensively.

Not with speculation.

Instead they communicate:

What we know.

What we don't know.

What we're investigating.

What actions are currently underway.

When the next update will be provided.

Good communication reduces confusion.

Confusion often becomes a second incident.

---

# Building a Hypothesis Instead of Guessing

When incidents begin, many engineers immediately search for answers.

Production engineers first build hypotheses.

For example:

The deployment introduced a regression.

The database is saturated.

The cache cluster is unavailable.

Traffic unexpectedly increased.

An external dependency is failing.

Each hypothesis produces specific questions.

Each question produces evidence.

Evidence eliminates incorrect assumptions.

The investigation gradually narrows until the actual cause becomes clear.

Guessing wastes time.

Hypothesis-driven investigation creates confidence.

---

# The Investigation Mindset

Production engineers think like investigators.

Every metric.

Every log.

Every trace.

Every deployment.

Every alert.

Every customer report.

Every infrastructure event.

Becomes a clue.

Rather than proving themselves right, they actively try to prove themselves wrong.

That discipline prevents confirmation bias.

Sometimes the first explanation appears convincing.

Experienced engineers continue validating until evidence supports the conclusion.

Production incidents reward curiosity.

Not ego.

---

# Mitigation vs Root Cause

One mistake many engineers make is trying to solve everything immediately.

Production engineers understand there are actually two separate objectives.

The first is mitigation.

Restore service.

Reduce customer impact.

Stabilize the system.

The second is identifying the root cause.

These are different activities.

Customers care about recovery.

Engineering cares about learning.

Recovery comes first.

Deep investigation follows afterward.

Confusing these two priorities often extends outages unnecessarily.

---

# Knowing When to Roll Back—and When Not To

Rolling back seems simple.

Until production becomes more complicated.

Sometimes rollback is the safest option.

Sometimes it creates even larger problems.

Consider situations involving:

Database schema changes.

Feature flag activation.

Background processing.

Data migrations.

External integrations.

Rolling back code may not restore previous data.

Production engineers evaluate rollback as carefully as deployment.

They ask:

Can rollback make the situation worse?

What dependencies changed?

Will rollback restore customer service?

Should we roll forward instead?

Rollback is a strategy.

Not a reflex.

---

# Working as One Incident Response Team

Production incidents are rarely solved by one engineer.

Infrastructure teams.

Application teams.

Database engineers.

Networking specialists.

Security teams.

Support.

Product owners.

Leadership.

Everyone has a role.

Production engineers avoid assigning blame during active incidents.

Instead they coordinate expertise.

They encourage information sharing.

They clarify responsibilities.

They remove confusion.

Incidents are solved faster when teams collaborate instead of competing.

---

# Blameless Postmortems and Continuous Learning

Once production has recovered, another important phase begins.

Learning.

Many organizations make the mistake of asking:

Who caused this?

Production engineering asks a different question.

Why did our system allow this to happen?

Blameless postmortems focus on improving systems.

Improving processes.

Improving automation.

Improving communication.

Improving documentation.

Improving monitoring.

The objective is not finding someone to blame.

It is preventing the next incident.

Organizations that learn from failure become more reliable over time.

Organizations that punish failure often repeat it.

---

# The Habits That Make Engineers Trusted During Outages

Trust is earned long before incidents occur.

Production engineers become trusted because they consistently demonstrate habits such as:

Preparing before emergencies.

Remaining calm during uncertainty.

Communicating honestly.

Making evidence-based decisions.

Protecting customers first.

Learning after every incident.

Helping teammates succeed.

Taking ownership without seeking credit.

These habits create confidence.

And confidence becomes trust.

---

# Key Takeaways

Every production engineer eventually experiences failure.

What separates great engineers is how they respond.

They understand that:

* Failure is inevitable in complex systems.
* Calm thinking produces better decisions.
* Investigation is more valuable than guessing.
* Communication is part of engineering.
* Restoring service comes before finding root causes.
* Collaboration solves incidents faster than individual heroics.
* Every outage is an opportunity to improve systems.
* Trust is earned through discipline, preparation, and consistent judgment.

The best production engineers are not remembered because nothing ever failed.

They are remembered because people trusted them when failure mattered most.
